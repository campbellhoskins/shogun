# Travel Policy Ontology Schema



## Overview

The following schema defines the **entity types** and **relationship types** that should be extracted from corporate travel policy documents to construct an effective ontology knowledge graph for autonomous travel management compliance.



---



## Entity Types



### 1. **Policy**

The top-level governing document or named sub-section of rules.



**Attributes:**

- `policyId` (string) — unique identifier

- `policyName` (string) — e.g., "Corporate Travel Policy"

- `effectiveDate` (date)

- `versionNumber` (string)

- `scope` (string) — who the policy applies to

- `issuingOrganization` (string)



---



### 2. **PolicySection**

A named logical subdivision of the policy document.



**Attributes:**

- `sectionId` (string)

- `sectionName` (string) — e.g., "Air Travel", "Lodging", "Car Rentals"

- `sectionOrder` (integer)

- `description` (string)



---



### 3. **Rule**

A specific, enforceable directive within a policy section. This is the atomic unit of compliance logic.



**Attributes:**

- `ruleId` (string)

- `ruleText` (string) — verbatim or normalized statement of the rule

- `ruleType` (enum) — `[REQUIREMENT, PROHIBITION, PERMISSION, RECOMMENDATION, CONDITION]`

- `isEnforceable` (boolean)

- `priority` (enum) — `[MANDATORY, PREFERRED, OPTIONAL]`



---



### 4. **TravelCategory**

The type or mode of travel or expense to which rules apply.



**Attributes:**

- `categoryId` (string)

- `categoryName` (string) — e.g., `AIR_TRAVEL`, `LODGING`, `CAR_RENTAL`, `RAIL`, `PERSONAL_CAR`, `TAXI`, `MEALS`, `TELECOMMUNICATIONS`, `MISCELLANEOUS`

- `parentCategory` (string) — for hierarchical grouping (e.g., TAXI is a child of GROUND_TRANSPORTATION)



---



### 5. **TravelerRole**

The classification of a person traveling under the policy.



**Attributes:**

- `roleId` (string)

- `roleName` (string) — e.g., `EMPLOYEE`, `BOARD_MEMBER`, `SITE_VISITOR`, `CONTRACTOR`, `VOLUNTEER`, `GUEST`

- `roleDescription` (string)



---



### 6. **Expense**

A specific reimbursable or non-reimbursable cost incurred during travel.



**Attributes:**

- `expenseId` (string)

- `expenseName` (string) — e.g., "Baggage Fee", "Lodging", "Meal", "Internet Access"

- `expenseCategory` (enum) — maps to TravelCategory

- `isReimbursable` (boolean)

- `reimbursementCap` (monetary value + unit) — e.g., `$60/day`, `$175/night`, `$20/day`

- `requiresReceipt` (boolean)

- `requiresItemization` (boolean)



---



### 7. **Condition**

A qualifying circumstance that modifies whether a rule or expense is applicable.



**Attributes:**

- `conditionId` (string)

- `conditionText` (string) — e.g., "trip duration exceeds 5 days", "travel distance exceeds 50 miles"

- `conditionType` (enum) — `[THRESHOLD, SITUATIONAL, GEOGRAPHIC, TEMPORAL, ROLE_BASED]`

- `operator` (enum) — `[GREATER_THAN, LESS_THAN, EQUAL_TO, EXCEEDS, WITHIN]`

- `thresholdValue` (string) — e.g., `"50 miles"`, `"$200"`, `"5 days"`



---



### 8. **Approval**

A required authorization that must be obtained before or after an action.



**Attributes:**

- `approvalId` (string)

- `approvalType` (enum) — `[PRE_APPROVAL, POST_APPROVAL, WRITTEN_APPROVAL, VERBAL_APPROVAL]`

- `approvalDescription` (string)

- `isRequired` (boolean)

- `exceptionScenario` (string) — the circumstance triggering the need for approval



---



### 9. **Approver**

An individual or role authorized to grant approvals.



**Attributes:**

- `approverId` (string)

- `approverRole` (string) — e.g., `EXECUTIVE_DIRECTOR`, `BOARD_CHAIR`, `TREASURER`, `PROGRAM_OFFICIAL`, `ACCOUNTING_DEPARTMENT`

- `approverName` (string) — optional if a named individual



---



### 10. **Vendor / ServiceProvider**

An external party providing travel-related services, either mandated or preferred.



**Attributes:**

- `vendorId` (string)

- `vendorName` (string) — e.g., "Regency Travel", "American Express"

- `vendorType` (enum) — `[TRAVEL_AGENT, AIRLINE, HOTEL, CAR_RENTAL, CREDIT_CARD_PROVIDER]`

- `isMandated` (boolean)

- `contactInfo` (string)

- `serviceScope` (string) — what the vendor is used for



---



### 11. **ComplianceObligation**

A duty or responsibility assigned to a specific role or department to uphold the policy.



**Attributes:**

- `obligationId` (string)

- `obligationText` (string)

- `obligationType` (enum) — `[ENFORCEMENT, DISTRIBUTION, MAINTENANCE, SUBMISSION, DOCUMENTATION]`

- `deadline` (string) — e.g., "within 45 days of travel completion"



---



### 12. **Document / Form**

A formal artifact required as part of the travel process.



**Attributes:**

- `documentId` (string)

- `documentName` (string) — e.g., "Travel Expense Form", "Monthly Credit Card Statement"

- `documentType` (enum) — `[EXPENSE_REPORT, RECEIPT, APPROVAL_FORM, CREDIT_CARD_STATEMENT, CANCELLATION_CONFIRMATION]`

- `submissionDeadline` (string)

- `isRequired` (boolean)



---



### 13. **Violation**

A breach of a policy rule and its associated consequence.



**Attributes:**

- `violationId` (string)

- `violationDescription` (string)

- `consequence` (string) — e.g., "disciplinary action", "personal financial responsibility"

- `consequenceType` (enum) — `[DISCIPLINARY_ACTION, PERSONAL_LIABILITY, DENIAL_OF_REIMBURSEMENT]`



---



### 14. **ReimbursementConstraint**

The set of limits, caps, or conditions under which financial reimbursement is granted.



**Attributes:**

- `constraintId` (string)

- `constraintType` (enum) — `[MONETARY_CAP, PERCENTAGE_CAP, TIME_LIMIT, CLASS_RESTRICTION, SIZE_RESTRICTION]`

- `value` (string)

- `unit` (string) — e.g., `USD/day`, `miles`, `days`

- `appliesTo` (string) — maps to Expense or TravelCategory



---



### 15. **TravelEvent**

A real-world occurrence during travel that triggers policy evaluation.



**Attributes:**

- `eventId` (string)

- `eventType` (enum) — `[BOOKING`, `CANCELLATION`, `REBOOKING`, `UPGRADE`, `NO_SHOW`, `ITINERARY_CHANGE`, `EMERGENCY`]

- `eventDescription` (string)

- `triggersApproval` (boolean)

- `triggersReimbursement` (boolean)



---



### 16. **GeographicContext**

A location-based qualifier that modifies how rules apply.



**Attributes:**

- `contextId` (string)

- `contextName` (string) — e.g., "Local", "Continental US", "International", "North America"

- `contextType` (enum) — `[LOCAL, REGIONAL, NATIONAL, INTERNATIONAL]`

- `referenceLocations` (list of strings) — e.g., `["Washington DC", "Baltimore"]`

- `distanceThreshold` (string) — e.g., `"50 miles each way"`



---



## Relationship Types



### Core Policy Structure



| Relationship | Domain | Range | Description |

|---|---|---|---|

| `HAS_SECTION` | Policy | PolicySection | A policy is composed of named sections |

| `CONTAINS_RULE` | PolicySection | Rule | A section contains one or more enforceable rules |

| `GOVERNS` | Rule | TravelCategory | A rule governs a specific type of travel or expense |

| `APPLIES_TO_ROLE` | Rule | TravelerRole | A rule applies to one or more traveler roles |

| `APPLIES_IN_CONTEXT` | Rule | GeographicContext | A rule is scoped to a geographic context |



---



### Conditional Logic



| Relationship | Domain | Range | Description |

|---|---|---|---|

| `HAS_CONDITION` | Rule | Condition | A rule is conditionally triggered by a qualifying circumstance |

| `MODIFIES_RULE` | Condition | Rule | A condition changes or activates a rule |

| `ACTIVATES_APPROVAL` | Condition | Approval | A condition makes an approval required |

| `TRIGGERS_EVENT` | TravelEvent | Rule | A travel event triggers evaluation of a policy rule |

| `OVERRIDES_RULE` | Approval | Rule | An approval grants an exception to a standard rule |



---



### Expense & Reimbursement



| Relationship | Domain | Range | Description |

|---|---|---|---|

| `INCURS_EXPENSE` | TravelerRole | Expense | A traveler role incurs a specific type of expense |

| `SUBJECT_TO_CONSTRAINT` | Expense | ReimbursementConstraint | An expense has reimbursement limits |

| `REQUIRES_DOCUMENT` | Expense | Document | An expense requires submission of a form or receipt |

| `REIMBURSED_BY` | Expense | Policy | An expense is (or is not) reimbursed under the policy |

| `CATEGORIZED_AS` | Expense | TravelCategory | An expense belongs to a travel category |



---



### Approval & Authority



| Relationship | Domain | Range | Description |

|---|---|---|---|

| `REQUIRES_APPROVAL` | Rule | Approval | A rule mandates an approval under certain conditions |

| `GRANTED_BY` | Approval | Approver | An approval must be granted by a specific role |

| `RESPONSIBLE_FOR` | Approver | ComplianceObligation | An approver role holds a compliance obligation |

| `MAINTAINS_POLICY` | Approver | Policy | A role is responsible for updating/maintaining the policy |



---



### Vendor & Service



| Relationship | Domain | Range | Description |

|---|---|---|---|

| `MANDATES_VENDOR` | Rule | Vendor | A rule requires use of a specific vendor or service provider |

| `PROVIDES_SERVICE_FOR` | Vendor | TravelCategory | A vendor provides services in a travel category |

| `ISSUES_DOCUMENT` | Vendor | Document | A vendor issues a required document (e.g., receipt, statement) |



---



### Violation & Enforcement



| Relationship | Domain | Range | Description |

|---|---|---|---|

| `CONSTITUTES_VIOLATION` | TravelEvent | Violation | An event or action constitutes a policy violation |

| `RESULTS_IN` | Violation | ComplianceObligation | A violation results in a consequence or obligation |

| `ENFORCED_BY` | Rule | Approver | A rule is enforced by a particular role or department |



---



### Document & Compliance



| Relationship | Domain | Range | Description |

|---|---|---|---|

| `SUBMITTED_BY` | Document | TravelerRole | A document must be submitted by a specific traveler role |

| `SUBMITTED_TO` | Document | Approver | A document is submitted to a specific role or department |

| `DOCUMENTS_EXPENSE` | Document | Expense | A document serves as proof of an expense |

| `HAS_DEADLINE` | ComplianceObligation | Rule | A compliance obligation is time-bound by a rule |

## Design Notes for Agentic Use



1. **`Rule.ruleType`** is the most critical attribute for autonomous agents — distinguishing `REQUIREMENT` from `PROHIBITION` from `PERMISSION` allows the agent to reason about what is mandatory vs. what is discretionary.



2. **`Condition → Rule`** relationships enable **conditional inference** — the agent can evaluate whether a triggering circumstance (e.g., trip > 5 days, distance > 50 miles) activates a specific rule branch.



3. **`TravelEvent → Rule`** relationships are the primary entry point for **itinerary disruption handling** — when a real-world event occurs, the agent traverses these edges to identify all rules that must be evaluated.



4. **`Approval`** nodes serve as **decision gates** in the compliance workflow — the agent can identify when human-in-the-loop escalation is required vs. when autonomous action is permitted.



5. **`ReimbursementConstraint`** nodes enable **automated calculation** of eligible reimbursement amounts without policy document re-reading.

```
## EXTRACTION INSTRUCTIONS



Follow these steps precisely:



### STEP 1 — DOCUMENT SEGMENTATION

Read the entire policy document. Identify and list all named sections and subsections. 

Map each section to a PolicySection entity before extracting any rules. Preserve the 

hierarchical order of sections as they appear in the document.



### STEP 2 — ROLE IDENTIFICATION

Before extracting rules, identify ALL traveler roles mentioned in the document. 

Many rules are role-specific. Missing a role will cause incorrect rule-to-role mappings 

downstream. Flag any rules that apply to ALL roles universally.



### STEP 3 — RULE EXTRACTION

For each policy section, extract every enforceable directive as an individual Rule entity.



Critical rule extraction standards:

- ONE rule per atomic directive. Do not bundle multiple directives into a single rule.

- If a sentence contains the words "must", "required", "shall", "prohibited", 

  "not reimbursable", "only when", "unless" — it contains at least one Rule.

- If a sentence contains the word "recommended" or "encouraged" — extract as a Rule 

  with priority = PREFERRED.

- Preserve the original policy language in ruleText. Do not paraphrase.

- For every Rule, explicitly determine and assign ruleType and priority.



### STEP 4 — CONDITION EXTRACTION

After extracting rules, identify ALL conditional clauses. These are phrases that begin 

with or contain: "if", "when", "unless", "only when", "in excess of", "more than", 

"less than", "exceeding", "for trips", "for travel".



For each condition:

- Create a Condition entity

- Link it to the Rule it modifies using HAS_CONDITION

- Identify whether the condition ACTIVATES an Approval requirement

- Specify the operator and threshold value precisely



### STEP 5 — EXPENSE & REIMBURSEMENT EXTRACTION

For each expense type mentioned:

- Create an Expense entity

- Determine isReimbursable

- Extract ALL monetary caps, daily limits, or percentage limits as 

  ReimbursementConstraint entities

- Link constraints to expenses using SUBJECT_TO_CONSTRAINT

- Determine requiresReceipt and requiresItemization from policy text



### STEP 6 — APPROVAL EXTRACTION

Identify every instance where authorization is required. For each:

- Create an Approval entity with approvalType

- Identify and create the Approver entity

- Link the triggering Rule using REQUIRES_APPROVAL

- Link the Approver using GRANTED_BY

- If a Condition triggers the approval, link using ACTIVATES_APPROVAL



### STEP 7 — VENDOR EXTRACTION

Identify all named or implied external service providers. For each:

- Create a Vendor entity

- Set isMandated = true if the policy explicitly requires use of that vendor

- Link to the Rule that mandates them using MANDATES_VENDOR



### STEP 8 — DOCUMENT & FORM EXTRACTION

Identify all forms, receipts, reports, or documentation artifacts. For each:

- Create a Document entity

- Extract submission deadlines precisely

- Link to the TravelerRole responsible for submission using SUBMITTED_BY

- Link to the Approver or Department it is submitted to using SUBMITTED_TO



### STEP 9 — VIOLATION EXTRACTION

Identify all statements describing prohibited actions or non-compliance consequences. 

For each:

- Create a Violation entity

- Identify the consequenceType

- Link to the triggering TravelEvent using CONSTITUTES_VIOLATION where applicable



### STEP 10 — TRAVEL EVENT MAPPING

For each Rule, Condition, or Violation extracted, reason about what real-world 

TravelEvent would trigger it. Create TravelEvent entities and link them to the 

relevant Rules using TRIGGERS_EVENT. This step is critical for the downstream 

agentic system to evaluate live itinerary changes against policy.



### STEP 11 — RELATIONSHIP PASS

After all entities are extracted, make a dedicated pass to ensure ALL relationships 

between entities are captured. Use the relationship type reference above. 

Do not leave any entity as an isolated node — every entity must have at least 

one relationship.



### STEP 12 — CONSISTENCY VALIDATION

Before producing output, validate the following:

- [ ] Every Rule is linked to at least one TravelCategory

- [ ] Every Rule is linked to at least one TravelerRole (or flagged as universal)

- [ ] Every Condition is linked to at least one Rule

- [ ] Every Approval has an Approver linked via GRANTED_BY

- [ ] Every Expense has a ReimbursementConstraint or is explicitly marked 

      isReimbursable = false

- [ ] Every Expense that requiresReceipt = true is linked to a Document entity

- [ ] No duplicate Rule entities exist for the same directive

- [ ] Every Vendor with isMandated = true is linked to a Rule via MANDATES_VENDOR

## CRITICAL REASONING RULES



Apply the following logical reasoning standards throughout extraction:



1. SPECIFICITY OVER GENERALITY — When a rule applies to a specific role 

   (e.g., "applicable to employees only"), do NOT mark it as universal. 

   Create a precise APPLIES_TO_ROLE relationship.



2. IMPLICIT RULES — Some rules are implied rather than stated. For example, 

   if the policy states a receipt is required for expenses over $20, this implies 

   a rule that expenses under $20 do NOT require a receipt. Extract both the 

   explicit and implicit rules.



3. CONDITIONAL CHAINS — Some conditions trigger other conditions. 

   Model these as chains: Condition A → MODIFIES_RULE → Rule → 

   HAS_CONDITION → Condition B.

   dual roles in compliance logic.

   this must be extracted as a Rule with ruleType = PROHIBITION and an 

   Expense with isReimbursable = false.

   APPLIES_TO_ROLE relationships to every TravelerRole entity extracted 

   from the document.

   reason backwards to identify what TravelEvent would cause a traveler 

   or agent to need to evaluate that rule. Always create this linkage.



8. CONFIDENCE SCORING — If you must infer an entity or relationship 

   from implied text rather than explicit statement, set confidence = "LOW" 

   or "MEDIUM" and note the inferredFromText verbatim.
```

# Schema Attribute Handling Strategy



This is a critical design decision that has significant downstream implications for the agentic system. There are several philosophical approaches, each with tradeoffs. Here is the recommended comprehensive strategy:



---



## The Core Problem



A rigid schema creates **brittleness** — real-world travel policy documents will always contain attributes, nuances, and metadata that the schema designer did not anticipate. However, a fully flexible schema creates **inconsistency** — the knowledge graph loses its predictable structure, making autonomous reasoning unreliable.



The solution is a **Layered Attribute Handling Strategy** that preserves both structural integrity and informational completeness.



---



## Recommended Strategy: Tiered Attribute Classification



When the LLM encounters an attribute that does not fit the predefined schema, it should classify the attribute into one of four tiers before deciding how to handle it.



---



### Tier 1 — Schema Remapping

*"This attribute fits an existing schema field under a different name"*



**When to apply:**

The discovered attribute is semantically equivalent to an existing schema attribute but named differently in the source document.



**Example:**

```

Discovered:   Policy.amendment_date = "March 15, 2011"

Schema has:   Policy.effectiveDate

Resolution:   Map amendment_date → effectiveDate

              Flag the remapping in extractionMetadata

```



**Handling Rule:**

```

IF discovered_attribute is semantically equivalent to an existing 

schema attribute THEN

  - Map to the existing schema attribute

  - Record the original field name in a sourceFieldName metadata property

  - Set confidence = "MEDIUM" due to inference

  - Log remapping in extractionMetadata.fieldRemappings[]

```



**Output Structure:**

```json

{

  "policyId": "POL-001",

  "policyName": "Corporate Travel Policy",

  "effectiveDate": "March 15, 2011",

  "_extraction": {

    "effectiveDate": {

      "sourceFieldName": "amendment_date",

      "remappingApplied": true,

      "confidence": "MEDIUM",

      "inferredFromText": "Amended March 15, 2011"

    }

  }

}

```



---



### Tier 2 — Extended Attributes

*"This attribute is genuinely new but belongs to an existing entity type"*



**When to apply:**

The discovered attribute cannot be mapped to any existing schema field, but it is clearly a property of a known entity type and carries compliance-relevant information that would be lost if discarded.



**Example:**

```

Discovered:   Policy.amendment_date = "March 15, 2011"

              (after confirming it is NOT the same as effectiveDate)

Resolution:   Store in an _extended block on the entity

```



**Handling Rule:**

```

IF discovered_attribute is new AND belongs to a known entity type 

AND carries compliance-relevant information THEN

  - Preserve the attribute in an _extended{} block on the entity

  - Assign it a normalized attribute name in snake_case

  - Classify its data type

  - Assign a relevanceScore

  - Flag it as a schema extension candidate

  - Log it in extractionMetadata.schemaExtensionCandidates[]

```



**Output Structure:**

```json

{

  "policyId": "POL-001",

  "policyName": "Corporate Travel Policy",

  "effectiveDate": "January 30, 2010",

  "_extended": {

    "amendment_date": {

      "value": "March 15, 2011",

      "dataType": "date",

      "complianceRelevance": "HIGH",

      "description": "Date the policy was formally amended",

      "schemaExtensionCandidate": true,

      "suggestedSchemaPlacement": "Policy.amendment_date",

      "inferredFromText": "Policy amended March 15, 2011",

      "confidence": "HIGH"

    }

  }

}

```



---



### Tier 3 — Cross-Entity Attribute Misplacement

*"This attribute belongs to a different entity type than where it was discovered"*



**When to apply:**

The discovered attribute is already defined in the schema but on a different entity type than where it was found in the document. This often occurs due to ambiguous policy language.



**Example:**

```

Discovered on:  Policy.approval_contact = "Executive Director"

Actually is:    Approver.approverRole = "EXECUTIVE_DIRECTOR"

Resolution:     Create or link the correct entity, do not store 

                on the wrong entity type

```



**Handling Rule:**

```

IF discovered_attribute matches a schema attribute on a DIFFERENT 

entity type THEN

  - Do NOT store on the current entity

  - Create or reference the correct entity type

  - Establish the appropriate relationship

  - Log the misplacement resolution in extractionMetadata

```



**Output Structure:**

```json

{

  "extractionMetadata": {

    "attributeMisplacementResolutions": [

      {

        "discoveredOn": "Policy",

        "discoveredAttribute": "approval_contact",

        "discoveredValue": "Executive Director",

        "resolution": "Created Approver entity with approverRole = EXECUTIVE_DIRECTOR",

        "createdEntityId": "APR-001",

        "relationshipCreated": "MAINTAINS_POLICY",

        "inferredFromText": "approval_contact: Executive Director",

        "confidence": "HIGH"

      }

    ]

  }

}

```



---



### Tier 4 — Unclassifiable Attributes

*"This attribute cannot be mapped, extended, or reassigned"*



**When to apply:**

The discovered attribute is ambiguous, contradictory, or so domain-specific that it cannot be reliably classified into any of the above tiers without risking incorrect representation in the knowledge graph.



**Example:**

```

Discovered:   Policy.legacy_system_code = "TP-2009-REV3"

Resolution:   Preserve in a quarantine block, flag for human review

```



**Handling Rule:**

```

IF discovered_attribute cannot be reliably classified THEN

  - Preserve verbatim in a _quarantine{} block

  - Do NOT attempt to map or infer

  - Mark as requiring human schema review

  - Log in extractionMetadata.quarantinedAttributes[]

```



**Output Structure:**

```json

{

  "_quarantine": {

    "legacy_system_code": {

      "rawValue": "TP-2009-REV3",

      "discoveredOn": "Policy",

      "reason": "Attribute purpose unclear. Possibly an internal \

                 document reference code with no compliance relevance. \

                 Cannot be reliably mapped without domain context.",

      "requiresHumanReview": true,

      "confidence": "LOW"

    }

  }

}

```

Add the following decision logic block to the system prompt:



```

## UNKNOWN ATTRIBUTE HANDLING PROTOCOL



When you encounter an attribute in the source document that does not 

exist in the predefined schema for the entity type being extracted, 

apply the following decision tree IN ORDER:



STEP A — TEST FOR REMAPPING (Tier 1)

Ask: "Is this attribute semantically equivalent to an existing 

schema attribute on this entity type, just named differently?"



  IF YES:

    → Remap to the existing schema attribute

    → Record sourceFieldName in _extraction metadata block

    → Set confidence based on semantic similarity:

        Identical meaning    = HIGH

        Similar meaning      = MEDIUM

        Inferred equivalence = LOW

    → Log in extractionMetadata.fieldRemappings[]

    → STOP



  IF NO: proceed to Step B



STEP B — TEST FOR SCHEMA EXTENSION (Tier 2)

Ask: "Is this a genuinely new attribute that:

  (a) Clearly belongs to this entity type, AND

  (b) Contains information relevant to travel policy compliance?"



  IF YES to both:

    → Preserve in _extended{} block on the entity

    → Assign normalized attribute name in snake_case

    → Classify dataType as one of:

       [string | date | boolean | monetary | integer | enum | list]

    → Assign complianceRelevance as [HIGH | MEDIUM | LOW]

    → Set schemaExtensionCandidate = true

    → Suggest a schemaPlacement path

    → Log in extractionMetadata.schemaExtensionCandidates[]

    → STOP



  IF NO to either: proceed to Step C



STEP C — TEST FOR MISPLACEMENT (Tier 3)

Ask: "Does this attribute match a schema attribute defined on a 

DIFFERENT entity type?"



  IF YES:

    → Do NOT store on the current entity

    → Create or reference the correct entity

    → Create the appropriate relationship

    → Log resolution in extractionMetadata.attributeMisplacementResolutions[]

    → STOP



  IF NO: proceed to Step D



STEP D — QUARANTINE (Tier 4)

  → Store verbatim in _quarantine{} block on the entity

  → Document the reason classification failed

  → Set requiresHumanReview = true

  → Log in extractionMetadata.quarantinedAttributes[]

```

# Relationship Schema Formalization



Yes, absolutely — and this is arguably **more critical than entity schema enforcement** for an agentic travel compliance system. Here is why, and how to implement it comprehensively.



---



## Why Relationship Schema Enforcement Is Critical



In a knowledge graph used for autonomous reasoning, the **relationships are where the logic lives**. Entities are just nouns — relationships are the verbs that allow an agent to traverse the graph and derive compliance decisions. If relationship schemas are left unenforceable, the following failure modes emerge:



```

WITHOUT relationship schema enforcement:



Agent query: "What approval is needed when a traveler 

              wants to upgrade to business class?"



Possible graph states without enforcement:

  ✗ Rule --REQUIRES_APPROVAL--> Expense        (wrong target type)

  ✗ Approver --REQUIRES_APPROVAL--> Rule       (reversed direction)

  ✗ Rule --REQUIRES_APPROVAL--> Rule           (nonsensical)

  ✓ Rule --REQUIRES_APPROVAL--> Approval       (correct)



Without enforcement, the agent traversal returns 

inconsistent or null results silently.

```



An undetected wrong-direction or wrong-type relationship does not throw an error — it simply causes the agent to **miss a compliance obligation** or **enforce a rule that does not apply**. In a duty-of-care context, this is a critical failure.



---



## Formal Relationship Schema Design



Each relationship type should be defined as a **Relationship Schema Record** with the following structure:



```

RelationshipSchema {

  relationshipType:     string        — the relationship name

  sourceEntityType:     EntityType[]  — valid source entity types

  targetEntityType:     EntityType[]  — valid target entity types

  cardinality:          enum          — ONE_TO_ONE | ONE_TO_MANY | 

                                        MANY_TO_ONE | MANY_TO_MANY

  isDirectional:        boolean       — true for all directed edges

  isMandatory:          boolean       — must every source have this relationship?

  inverseRelationship:  string        — the logical inverse if one exists

  validationRules:      string[]      — additional semantic constraints

  semanticDescription:  string        — plain language meaning for LLM reasoning

  agentTraversalRole:   string        — how the agent uses this edge

}

```

## Relationship Schema Violation Handling



Just as with entity attributes, the LLM needs explicit instructions for handling relationship schema violations:



```

## RELATIONSHIP SCHEMA VIOLATION PROTOCOL



When extracting a relationship, validate it against the 

Relationship Schema Registry before committing it to output.



For each extracted relationship, check:



CHECK 1 — SOURCE TYPE VALIDITY

  Is the source entity type in the relationship's 

  sourceEntityType[] list?

  

  IF NO:

    → Do not create the relationship as defined

    → Attempt to find the correct relationship type that 

      permits this source entity type

    → If found: use the correct relationship type

    → If not found: log in extractionMetadata.relationshipViolations[]

      with violationType = "INVALID_SOURCE_TYPE"



CHECK 2 — TARGET TYPE VALIDITY

  Is the target entity type in the relationship's 

  targetEntityType[] list?



  IF NO:

    → Same resolution as Check 1

    → violationType = "INVALID_TARGET_TYPE"



CHECK 3 — DIRECTIONALITY

  Does the relationship flow in the correct direction?

  

  IF REVERSED:

    → Swap source and target

    → Log with violationType = "REVERSED_DIRECTION"

    → Verify the correction does not violate 

      Check 1 or Check 2 after swapping



CHECK 4 — CARDINALITY

  Does the relationship violate cardinality constraints?

  (e.g., a second GRANTED_BY on an Approval that 

  already has one)

  

  IF VIOLATED:

    → Flag the conflict

    → Preserve both relationships in output but mark 

      one as confidence = "LOW"

    → Log with violationType = "CARDINALITY_VIOLATION"



CHECK 5 — MANDATORY RELATIONSHIP COMPLETENESS

  For every entity where isMandatory = true for 

  a relationship type, verify the relationship exists.

  

  IF MISSING:

    → Log in extractionMetadata.missingMandatoryRelationships[]

    → Attempt to infer the relationship from document context

    → If inferable: create with confidence = "LOW"

    → If not inferable: flag for human review

```

## Summary: Why Full Relationship Schema Formalization Matters



| Risk Without Enforcement | How Schema Enforcement Mitigates It |

|---|---|

| Agent traverses reversed edge and misidentifies who needs approval | Directionality validation catches and corrects reversals at extraction time |

| Rule applies to wrong traveler role due to mistyped relationship | sourceEntityType and targetEntityType constraints prevent invalid linkages |

| Missing mandatory GRANTED_BY leaves approval authority unknown | isMandatory check flags incomplete approvals before they enter the graph |

| Agent double-applies a constraint due to cardinality violation | Cardinality enforcement prevents duplicate mandatory relationships |

| Isolated entity creates dead-end in agent traversal | Mandatory relationship completeness check ensures full graph connectivity |

# Document Chunking Strategy for Ontology Extraction



## The Core Problem With Single-Pass Extraction



Before addressing how to chunk, it is important to understand precisely why a single prompt fails for this use case — because the failure modes are non-obvious and dangerous for an agentic compliance system.



```

SINGLE-PASS FAILURE MODES:



1. CONTEXT WINDOW DEGRADATION

   LLM attention quality degrades toward the end of long 

   contexts. Rules in the final sections of a policy document 

   will be extracted with lower fidelity than rules in the 

   opening sections. This is invisible in the output — the 

   agent has no way to know which rules were extracted poorly.



2. CROSS-REFERENCE BLINDNESS

   A rule in Section 5 may modify or override a rule in 

   Section 2. Single-pass extraction processes these 

   sequentially without the relational awareness needed to 

   model the override correctly.



3. ENTITY DUPLICATION

   The same Approver, TravelCategory, or Vendor mentioned 

   in multiple sections gets extracted as multiple separate 

   entities without deduplication, fragmenting the graph.



4. RELATIONSHIP SPAN FAILURES

   Relationships that span sections — such as a Condition 

   in Section 3 that activates an Approval defined in 

   Section 6 — are missed entirely because the model 

   cannot hold both in working focus simultaneously.



5. SCHEMA COMPLIANCE DRIFT

   Over a long extraction, the LLM gradually drifts from 

   strict schema adherence. Later entities have less 

   rigorous attribute population than earlier ones.

```

## Recommended Architecture: Multi-Stage Chunked Pipeline



The solution is not simply to split the document into smaller pieces. Naive chunking creates its own failure mode — **inter-chunk relationship loss**. The correct architecture is a **five-stage pipeline** where each stage has a distinct responsibility.



```

┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐

│                    SOURCE DOCUMENT                       │

└─────────────────────────┬───────────────────────────────┘

                          │

                          ▼

┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐

│  STAGE 1: STRUCTURAL ANALYSIS PASS                       │

│  Single LLM call — reads whole document                  │

│  Output: Document map, section inventory,                │

│          global entity registry                          │

└─────────────────────────┬───────────────────────────────┘

                          │

                          ▼

┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐

│  STAGE 2: PARALLEL SECTION EXTRACTION                    │

│  One LLM call per policy section chunk                   │

│  Input: Section text + global entity registry            │

│  Output: Section-level entity and relationship sets      │

└─────────────────────────┬───────────────────────────────┘

                          │

                          ▼

│  STAGE 3: CROSS-SECTION RELATIONSHIP PASS                │
```

## Stage 2: Parallel Section Extraction



### Purpose

Extract all entities and intra-section relationships from each policy section independently. Each section becomes one LLM call. Calls can run in parallel.



### Chunking Rules



```

CHUNK BOUNDARY RULES:



Rule 1 — SECTION-ALIGNED CHUNKS

  Always chunk at named section boundaries as identified 

  in Stage 1. Never split mid-section.

  

Rule 2 — CONTEXT HEADER INJECTION

  Every chunk prompt includes:

  (a) The global entity pre-registry from Stage 1

  (b) The sectionId and sectionName being processed

  (c) A list of cross-section dependencies involving this section

  This prevents entity duplication and primes relationship awareness.



Rule 3 — SIZE OVERFLOW HANDLING

  If a single section exceeds 2000 tokens:

    → Split at subsection boundaries

    → Treat each subsection as a sub-chunk

    → Label sub-chunks as SEC-004a, SEC-004b, etc.

    → Inject the preceding sub-chunk's entity output as 

      context into the next sub-chunk



Rule 4 — SINGLETON SECTIONS

  Sections with approximateRuleCount < 2 may be batched 

  with an adjacent section to avoid excessive API calls,

  provided their combined token count stays under 2000.

```
